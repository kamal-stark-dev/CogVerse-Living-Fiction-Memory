"Ozymandias" — named for the Shelley poem about the ruin of a once-great empire — is widely considered the greatest single episode of Breaking Bad and one of the finest hours in television history. It depicts the complete collapse of everything Walter White had built: his money, his freedom, his family, and his self-image as a man who had protected the people he loved.

The episode began in the aftermath of the Tohajiilee shootout. Hank had arrested Walt at the burial site, and Walt had called Jack Welker's gang in a panic — and then immediately tried to call them off when he saw Jesse was with Hank and Gomez. Jack's gang arrived heavily armed. A gunfight erupted. Steven Gomez was killed. Hank, wounded, crawled toward a fallen shotgun only to have it taken away.

Jack prepared to execute Hank. Walt emerged from the SUV and begged for Hank's life, revealing the location of his $80 million to bargain with. Hank looked at Walt and said, without cruelty, "You're the smartest guy I ever met, and you're too stupid to see — he made up his mind ten minutes ago." Jack shot Hank through the forehead. Walt fell to the ground, sobbing. The man who had manipulated everything — who had outmaneuvered Tuco, Gus, the DEA, the cartel — could not save his own brother-in-law.

Jack's gang then dug up the buried barrels of cash — approximately $80 million — and took all but one, leaving Walt a single barrel as Todd's gesture of apology. Under the car, they discovered Jesse hiding. Before Jack could execute Jesse, Todd intervened, suggesting they keep Jesse alive to learn what he had told the DEA. Walt, in a final act of cruelty, told Jesse — before the gang dragged him away — that he had watched Jane die and chose not to save her.

Walt drove home in a truck he bought from a Navajo man with $10,000 in cash, a bullet hole in the gas tank making a trail across the desert. He arrived at the house and tried to pack his family and flee. Skyler refused. She reached for a kitchen knife. Walt grabbed her. Walter White Jr. tackled his father, called 911, and reported that his father was dangerous. In the chaos, Walt grabbed Holly and drove away.

He called Skyler from a payphone — a staged call, knowing the police were listening, knowing Skyler understood he was performing for them. He screamed at her, threatened her, took full blame upon himself in the most extravagant possible terms. He was giving her an alibi. She understood. The call ended. Walt left Holly at a fire station for Skyler to reclaim.

The next day, Walt waited at the edge of a road with the last barrel of money — approximately $11 million — for Ed Galbraith, the disappearer. The car arrived. Walt packed his things. He drove away from Albuquerque and everyone he had ever loved, into a new identity as Mr. Lambert in a New Hampshire cabin, alone.

The episode title derives from Shelley's poem: "Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair! / Nothing beside remains." All that was left of Heisenberg's empire was a scorched site in the desert, two DEA agents dead in the dirt, and a man rolling a barrel of money through the New Mexico scrub while his family fell apart behind him.
